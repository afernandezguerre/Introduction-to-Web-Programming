MISUSE OF AI ON SOFTWARE ENGINEERING DEV(OPS) PROCEDURE

Alejandro Fernández Guerrero - 003346075

 

INTRODUCTION:

 

 

 

DevOps raised back during 2007-2009. His creator, Patrick Debois defined this procedure as: “Everything you do to overcome the friction created by silos… the rest, is just plain engineering”. This research paper explores the ways in which AI would wrongly support or even replace humans in the development part of the DevOps procedure. Leaving exploration on the operational side and on more specialised procedures such as SecOps, MLOps or LLMOps..

 

Development operations on the DevOps model are composed by the following stages: Plan, Code, Build and Test. The DevOps lifecycle begins with Planning, where requirements are gathered and translated into a roadmap of tasks. This leads directly into Coding, where developers write and commit source code. Once committed, the Build phase automatically compiles this code into a functional software package, which is immediately moved into Testing to undergo automated quality and security checks.

Following successful tests, the software enters the Release stage to be staged for production. During Deployment, the update is pushed to live servers, ensuring the new features are available to users. The cycle moves into Operations, where the system is managed for stability, and Monitoring, where performance data is collected. This data is then fed back into the next Planning phase, closing the loop to begin the cycle of continuous improvement.

In the Planning phase, the legitimate use of AI focuses on predictive analytics for backlog management and risk mitigation by processing historical velocity data. However, misuse occurs when strategic vision is delegated to "black-box" models. Over-reliance on AI to define roadmaps can strip away the complexities of organizational and technical context, transforming planning into a rigid process that perpetuates historical biases and leaves no room for the creative breakthrough required for architectural innovation.

 

Regarding the use of Artificial Intelligence (AI) within the Scrum framework, specifically in the planning and requirements analysis stages, the following issues are identified:

In the planning stage, the primary challenge lies in the high level of uncertainty and the difficulty of producing accurate cost and resource estimates. AI user stories are inherently less predictable than traditional software development tasks, which complicates the definition of clear objectives during Sprint Planning and can create stressful environments for teams unfamiliar with such iterative volatility. Furthermore, integrating AI tools for agile project management faces significant hurdles regarding transparency and "explainability", as automated decisions regarding task allocation or deadline estimation often lack a logic that is easily comprehensible to human team members (Ameta et al.).

 

Regarding requirements analysis, the problem centers on the complexity of translating customer needs into viable technical specifications for data models. The process of AI product discovery is intricate and requires heavy experimentation, alongside the coordination of highly diverse teams—including data scientists, analysts, and developers—to identify valid use cases before refining them into the Product Backlog. Additionally, the quality of requirements is compromised if the historical data used by the AI is biased or incomplete, which can lead to the erroneous prioritization of features and directly impact the value delivered to the client at the end of each iteration (Marques, silva and Bernardino, 2024).

Vibe Coding represents the pinnacle of abstraction, where the engineer interacts through natural language and design "intuition." While tools like GitHub Copilot accelerate the generation of boilerplate and refactoring, their misuse encourages "cognitive atrophy" in the developer. The risk lies in the injection of subtle security vulnerabilities and the accumulation of invisible technical debt; if code is generated based on "vibes" without rigorous validation of the underlying logic, it creates fragile systems that humans are no longer capable of manually debugging.

 

Even though vibe coding may seem effortless, the AI does not have any reasoning behind its answers. This is backed by the study of Danassis and Goel (2025), who made computer science students compete against LLMs to see the quality of both outputs. While vibe coding could work on small projects, the study showed that human code presented better quality than the one made by AI.

 

According to the study by Chou et al. (2025), the practice of vibe coding introduces several critical challenges to modern software projects, primarily centered around the shift from deterministic engineering to a probabilistic process described as "rolling the dice." A major issue is design fixation, where developers tend to accept or refine the initial AI-generated output rather than exploring more robust architectural alternatives, which can lead to suboptimal system designs. Furthermore, the reliance on natural language prompts often results in a lack of rigorous code inspection and unreliable evaluation practices; developers frequently delegate the verification of code quality back to the AI itself or rely on surface-level observations ("vibes") rather than technical validation. This approach creates significant production risks, such as hidden bugs and security vulnerabilities, as the opaque nature of AI tools hinders the development of accurate mental models, leaving developers unable to fully understand or control the underlying logic of the software they are building-

 

In the Building step (CI/CD), AI enables the optimization of compile times and intelligent dependency management, detecting anomalies in artifacts before deployment. Nonetheless, misuse happens when automation becomes so complex and opaque that DevOps engineers lose traceability of the build process. An "hallucination" in AI-generated Infrastructure as Code (IaC) configurations can compromise the integrity of the entire software supply chain without triggering traditional alarms.

 

According to Luz et al. (2024), integrating AI into CI/CD pipelines introduces significant problems, primarily centered on data dependencies, where a lack of large, high-quality datasets leads to inaccurate predictions and unreliable behavior. The authors highlight pipeline complexity and the "black-box" nature of AI models as major hurdles, as these make debugging and troubleshooting difficult for human operators. Furthermore, the text identifies severe security risks, such as susceptibility to adversarial attacks and automation errors that can trigger incorrect deployments or unintended rollbacks. Organizations also face human-centric issues, including the degradation of developer skills due to over-reliance on automation, model bias in decision-making, and high infrastructure costs that can diminish the long-term return on investment.

The Testing phase, traditionally the bottleneck of the SDLC (Software Development Life Cycle), currently faces a paradoxical crisis driven by AI integration. While LLMs can generate test suites at unprecedented speeds, this automation introduces the risk of "hallucinated coverage." Engineers often surrender to the "automation bias," assuming that a high volume of AI-generated test cases equates to a robust system. However, these tools frequently produce "shallow tests" that validate syntax or optimistic scenarios while failing to probe edge cases or complex state interactions. Furthermore, the use of AI in testing without human-in-the-loop verification leads to the Oracle Problem: the AI may generate a test based on its own misunderstood logic of the code, effectively "grading its own homework" and allowing logical vulnerabilities to propagate into production under the guise of 100% code coverage. (Moradi Dakhel et al., 2024).

 

According to all these problems, AI has performed the best (not perfect) on testing, building, and then coding and planning.

SOLUTION:

 

 

Currently it is even more common to see all kind of “gurus” or AI false prophets, granting that they don’t need any more programmers, developers and other IT derivatives for bringing their ideas into reality. However, this is far from true, specially in the software engineering industry. The client often lacks the knowledge to specify the AI what will be the inner components of the software application, the requirements that will be implemented, the testability and how to launch it for users. Not even some developers now how to explain the AI the things it needs to know for it to come up with the right answer.

 

With all these problem context in mind, we have got to create a solution for the software engineering development sector.  But for this, we have got to first, understand the capabilities of AI, knowing when and how to use it.

 

AI came not as a substitution of the human being, but as a college, that will help human beings to ask what are the essential characteristics of being a human: What makes us different from a machine?. The answer to this question has been already given thanks to the research made by Wu et al. (2024). AI presented better performing on tasks that were repetitive and lacked of complex rationale. Humans, on the other side, they performed the best on tasks related to creativity, simplicity over complexity, understanding context, team capabilities and emotions. In their study, AI has proven to outperform the human-being in outstanding ways. There are many examples such us: Cough tests for detecting Covid-19 by MIT, Alphafold on protein folding problem by DeepMind, Web App builders that work with words, Collaborative robots in assembly by University of Southern Denmark, etc. The AI can become a boost of our own capacities by following the “Human-AI Co-Creation Model” from Wu et al. (2024). In this model, AI helps us to: Perceive, Think, Express, Collaborate, Build, and Test ideas.

 

“The first phase is to perceive, where human perception can be enhanced by big data and sensors with AI. Beyond the Senses that humans normally perceive the world with, AI can turn big data into meaningful information and knowledge using all kinds of sensors and networks, giving human expanded views in both perceptual and rational perspectives. The second phase is to think, where humans can think deeper and wider with AI. Inspiration and exploration that AI brings can go far more than human considerations. This will break the limits of resource and help human think deeper, wider, in a more thorough but also efficient way, potentially leading to unexpected accomplishments. The third phase is to express, where humans can explore more and rapidly with AI. Various ideas and diverse people need their optimal ways to present, such as painting, designing, composing, writing, performing, coding, prototyping… Empowered by AI tools, people won’t be stopped for lack of talent or training. Creativity matters more than skills. The fourth phase is to collaborate, where human and AI play to each other’s strengths. Whether working alone or with others, people can always team up with AI. Just fully understand the strengths and limitations of both human and AI, and give each side the best assignment. The fifth phase and the sixth are to build and test, where production can achieve higher quality and lower cost by simulating and analyzing with AI. Rehearsing gives people a chance to predict how things will go and to prepare ourselves for real-world events. With detailed simulation and calculation offered by AI, the process and result of building and testing can be handled effectively and efficiently. During this creative process, human and AI can complement each other and unleash the great potential of both sides.”  (Wu et al., 2024, p.178)

 

Now, coming back to the problems that were exposed on the introduction of this document, we could highlight the following statement:

 

On Code Validation and the "Oracle Problem": To mitigate the risks of "hallucinated coverage" and the pitfalls of vibe coding, the solution must involve implementing automated evaluation frameworks that function independently of the generative model. As research into LLM-generated code quality suggests, integrating static and dynamic analysis tools acts as an objective oracle, ensuring that rapid code generation does not compromise the long-term maintainability or security of the software architecture (Alshammari et al., 2025).

 

 

Taking into consideration the previous information, it is needed a framework for AI DevOps, where developers can inform themselves about how and in which contexts they should be using certain AI models. Leaving the most repetitive procedures to AI, where it shines the best by its performance.

In the context of evolving agile methodologies, Ameta, Patel and Rathore propose an operational abstraction that integrates Artificial Intelligence (AI) with the Scrum framework, arguing that its iterative and incremental nature provides an optimal solution for managing the inherent uncertainty, heavy experimentation, and intricate product discovery required in AI development. Under this paradigm, the development lifecycle is articulated through the reconfiguration of traditional artifacts, such as the Product Backlog, which transitions into a dynamic repository capable of navigating not only standard user stories but also the rigorous data collection, preprocessing, and permutation demands necessary for model training (Ameta, Patel and Rathore). Recognizing that conventional software engineering methods are too rigid for such multidisciplinary efforts, the proposed framework relies on short, time-boxed Sprints that function as continuous validation cycles—encompassing everything from feature engineering and algorithm selection to post-processing validation. This structural integration acts as a continuous quality assurance mechanism, where frequent inspection via Daily Stand-Ups and systemic adaptability through Sprint Retrospectives allow cross-functional teams to iteratively refine probabilistic models and system architectures. Ultimately, by continuously testing small increments of the product, this agile fusion mitigates the risks of unpredictable outcomes, ensuring that practitioners can maintain rigorous technical scrutiny while delivering highly functional, value-driven software that maximizes operational cost efficiency and Return on Investment (Ameta, Patel and Rathore).

 

A possible solution for the vibe coding problem, could be related to the transition to vibe coding shifts on the software engineering paradigm from manual syntax construction to high-level natural language prompting, requiring practitioners to navigate the inherent stochasticity of generative models rather than treating them as infallible oracles. To manage this non-deterministic "rolling the dice" logic, the engineer must adopt the role of a semantic auditor, iteratively refining prompts to bridge the gap between abstract intent and verifiable architectural structures. This workflow optimization ensures that productivity gains from AI do not result in opaque or unmanageable codebases; instead, by applying rigorous technical scrutiny to probabilistic outputs, developers maintain the system reliability and architectural integrity essential for complex, mission-critical DevOps environments.

 

To address the friction inherent in autonomous software delivery, a key architectural solution lies in the implementation of AI-driven Predictive Pipeline Orchestration (PPO). This method replaces static, manual gatekeeping with dynamic, ML-powered evaluation layers that analyse real-time telemetry and historical commit metadata to assess risk before code ever reaches production. As Tereshchenko et al. (2024) emphasize, the most successful strategies for adopting such high-level automation involve university-industry collaboration to develop "Best Strategies" that prevent the pipeline from becoming an opaque "Black Box." By integrating these predictive agents into the CI/CD loop, organizations can transition toward a "Progressive Delivery" model, where the system autonomously manages canary releases and triggers immediate, telemetry-based rollbacks if deviations from baseline performance are detected. However, for such an autonomous system to maintain its integrity, it must be supported by a rigorous verification layer that ensures the automated outputs are functionally sound rather than merely syntactically correct.

 

 

To mitigate the risks in testing with AI, the focus must shift from mere test generation to rigorous test validation. The effectiveness of AI in this domain is not inherent to the model's size, but to the framework of its application. According to Hanif, Tawosi, and Sarro (2024), while pre-trained LLMs are powerful for initial test synthesis, their output requires a secondary layer of objective scrutiny. This is where MuTap (Mutation-based Test Augmentation) serves as a critical solution. By employing mutation testing—intentionally injecting small, controlled faults (mutants) into the source code—the framework evaluates whether the AI-generated tests are actually capable of detecting changes in logic. This synergy ensures that the "vibe" of the generated test is backed by empirical "killing power" over mutants. Consequently, the integration of MuTap transforms AI from a potential source of false security into a precision tool, bridging the gap between automated velocity and the uncompromising reliability required in modern software engineering.

 

In the end, these are particular solutions for particular problems. The solution, as previously stated, is not about individual solutions, but about gathering all of them into a cohesive paradigm.

 

To synthesize these phase-specific methodologies into a unified operational paradigm, this paper proposes the Cognitive DevOps Integration Framework (CDIF). The primary objective of CDIF is to redefine the foundational premise of the software delivery lifecycle in the algorithmic age. If, as Debois originally said, traditional DevOps was about overcoming the friction created by human organizational silos (Development and Operations), the CDIF architecture is designed to overcome the modern friction between human cognitive intent and the, non-deterministic nature of Artificial Intelligence outputs.

CDIF operates on the principle of "Bounded Autonomy," ensuring that AI acts as an operational accelerator within strict, human-governed deterministic boundaries. Rather than treating planning, coding, building, and testing as isolated stages where AI operates independently, the framework connects these solutions into a continuous, self-regulating feedback loop:

1. Dynamic Intent Formalization (Planning & Discovery) Within the modernized DevOps lifecycle, the initial planning and requirements gathering phase undergoes a structural shift from static documentation to dynamic intent formalization. Drawing upon modern agile integration practices, the framework replaces traditional, rigid requirements with a dynamic, probabilistic repository. However, to prevent generative models from straying into architectural drift, this framework mandates that all user stories and epics are embedded with explicit "semantic constraints." These constraints serve as strict architectural, ethical, and operational boundaries within which the AI is permitted to generate solutions. This pre-emptive bounding effectively limits design fixation before any Large Language Model (LLM) is prompted, shifting the human role from requirements dictation to constraint architecture.

      Principle: AI is not forbidden, but semantic boundaries are absolute (AI boosts, but humans correct the errors and missing information). User stories must act as strict architectural blueprints rather than linguistic suggestions.

2. Semantic Auditing and Deterministic Translation (Coding) As the lifecycle transitions into the coding phase, the traditional role of the software developer is fundamentally elevated. The risky phenomenon of relying purely on stochastic AI outputs—often referred to as "vibe coding"—is structurally regulated. The developer transitions from a primary syntax generator to a "Semantic Auditor." Utilizing the bounded constraints established in the planning phase, the auditor iteratively evaluates, refines, and steers natural language prompts. The framework dictates that AI-generated code, particularly boilerplate and repetitive logic, cannot proceed to the integration pipeline based merely on surface-level compilability (the structure of the code is one of the most important parts of an application as it sets up everything for future methods and updates). It must be actively mapped back to the original dynamic repository to ensure logical fidelity. This human-in-the-loop technical scrutiny ensures that stochastic outputs are translated into verifiable, deterministic architectural structures, mitigating the silent accumulation of invisible technical debt.

      Principle: Audit the logic, reject the stochastic "vibe". Developers must act as semantic gatekeepers, enforcing deterministic translation over passive syntax acceptance.

3. Automated Integrity & Supply Chain Provenance (Building) In the Building phase, the framework directly addresses the opacity of AI-generated artifacts by implementing stringent provenance verification layers. The reliance on external datasets and the inherent "black-box" nature of AI models can seamlessly introduce vulnerabilities into the software supply chain without triggering traditional heuristic alarms. Therefore, the framework mandates that AI-driven build tools do not merely optimize compilation times or manage dependencies, but rather generate a rigorous "Cognitive Integrity Manifesto." This artifact provides total traceability, delineating precisely which infrastructure-as-code (IaC) components and dependencies were suggested by probabilistic models and which are deterministic human inputs. By crossing these suggestions with the semantic constraints from the planning phase, the build process acts as a rigorous security filter, ensuring no infrastructure "hallucinations" or compromised dependencies reach the testing phase.

      Principle: Traceability supersedes speed. An automated build is structurally invalid if the origin, intent, and integrity of every generated dependency cannot be cryptographically proven.

4. Mutation-Driven Gatekeeping (Testing) Before any AI-assisted code is permitted to enter the release pipeline, the framework mandates rigorous empirical validation to counteract the limitations of standard testing metrics. The literature acknowledges that while code coverage is frequently used to assess generated tests, it is weakly correlated with the actual efficiency of a test suite in bug detection. To resolve this, the testing phase integrates Mutation-based Test case generation using Augmented Prompt [MuTAP (Moradi Dakhel et al., 2024)]. Rather than relying on passive syntax checking, MuTAP leverages mutation testing by injecting artificial changes—based on real faults—into the Program Under Test (PUT) to create "mutants".

 

If AI-generated test cases fail to detect these faults, the surviving mutants explicitly highlight the limitations and weaknesses of the test suite. MuTAP actively utilizes these surviving mutants to augment the prompt for the LLM, creating a closed feedback loop that forces the AI to iteratively generate new, highly effective test cases capable of "killing" the remaining mutants. This process, which also includes automated post-processing to fix syntactic and functional errors in the AI's output, transforms the testing suite into an aggressive, logic-validating gatekeeper. It ensures that the final tests possess genuine, empirically validated power to expose defects, rather than merely inflating code coverage metrics.

 

      Principle: Validate the validator. AI-generated tests must prove their empirical worth by catching intentional, mutation-driven faults, effectively gatekeeping the transition from development to deployment.

Ultimately, the CDIF model demonstrates that the integration of AI into software engineering is not a linear substitution of human labor, but a fundamental reconfiguration of the engineering mandate. By unifying dynamic agile planning, semantic auditing, and mutation-driven gatekeeping through MuTAP , the framework isolates repetitive, probabilistic tasks to the machine, while positioning the human engineer as the systemic governor. This holistic approach ensures that the unprecedented velocity of generative AI does not compromise the deterministic reliability required in modern development environments. By shifting the friction from manual syntax production to high-level semantic validation, the framework successfully resolves the emerging tensions within the contemporary software engineering lifecycle, transforming the threat of AI misuse into a structured evolution of the professional roles of IT.

 

DISCUSSION

The transition from traditional DevOps to the proposed Cognitive DevOps Integration Framework (CDIF) represents more than a technical upgrade; it is a socio-technical necessity in an era where "Vibe Coding" threatens to decouple engineering from actual reasoning. While current industry trends suggest a total displacement of the human element, the reality aligns more closely with the findings of Wu et al. (2024). The synergy between human creativity and machine velocity is not automatic; it requires a structured governance model that mitigates the "Black Box" problem inherent in Large Language Models. The more we use AI in a healthy and responsible way, the better our performing with it will be.

Furthermore, a robust AI-integrated DevOps procedure must address the challenge of energy consumption and resource optimization. A sustainable solution involves re-engineering the CI/CD pipeline to prioritize "green" software engineering, utilizing predictive AI models to minimize the carbon footprint of data centers and cloud-native deployments, ensuring that technological acceleration remains environmentally viable (Lwakatare, Karvonen and Abrahamsson, 2025). This environmental focus is particularly relevant in the context of Nordic research hubs, such as LUT University, where digitalization must align with carbon-neutrality goals.

CONCLUSION

The emergence of Generative AI has fundamentally disrupted the Software Engineering lifecycle, introducing a paradox where increased velocity often results in diminished structural integrity. This research has identified that the primary friction in modern DevOps is no longer just between departmental silos, but between human intent and the stochastic nature of machine logic. Through the exploration of the Cognitive DevOps Integration Framework (CDIF), this paper has demonstrated that the "Vibe Coding" crisis and the risks of "hallucinated coverage" can be mitigated by shifting the human role from manual execution to high-level semantic validation.

By unifying dynamic agile planning, semantic auditing, and empirical gatekeeping via mutation testing, CDIF transforms the threat of AI misuse into a structured evolution of the engineering profession. Ultimately, the future of (software) engineering lies not in the competition against AI, but in the masterful governance of it. By embracing "Bounded Autonomy," the global society can ensure a future where technology serves as a reliable boost of human wisdom, pushing the world toward a more sustainable and transparent digital reality.
